Vibrations · 27
Single Degree of Freedom (SDOF)

The fundamental vibration model — free vibration, damped response, forced vibration, transmissibility, and rotating imbalance.

Undamped free vibration

\[ m\ddot{x}+kx=0,\quad\omega_n=\sqrt{\frac{k}{m}},\quad f_n=\frac{\omega_n}{2\pi} \]
Natural frequency: \(\omega_n\) [rad/s], \(f_n\) [Hz]. Depends only on stiffness and mass. Increasing stiffness raises \(f_n\); increasing mass lowers it. General solution: \(x(t)=A\cos\omega_n t+B\sin\omega_n t\), where \(A\) and \(B\) are set by initial conditions.

Damped free vibration

\[ m\ddot{x}+c\dot{x}+kx=0 \]
Equation of motion with damping: \(c\) = damping coefficient [N·s/m]. Solution depends on the damping ratio \(\zeta\).
\[ c_c=2\sqrt{km}=2m\omega_n,\quad\zeta=\frac{c}{c_c} \]
Critical damping and damping ratio: \(c_c\) = critical damping coefficient [N·s/m] — minimum damping for no oscillation. \(\zeta\) = damping ratio [dimensionless]:
  • \(\zeta < 1\): underdamped — oscillatory decay (most real structures)
  • \(\zeta = 1\): critically damped — returns to rest as fast as possible without oscillating
  • \(\zeta > 1\): overdamped — slow exponential decay without oscillation
\[ \omega_d=\omega_n\sqrt{1-\zeta^2},\quad x(t)=Xe^{-\zeta\omega_n t}\sin(\omega_d t+\phi) \]
Damped natural frequency \(\omega_d\): Always less than \(\omega_n\). For lightly damped systems (\(\zeta < 0.1\)): \(\omega_d \approx \omega_n\). The exponential envelope \(Xe^{-\zeta\omega_n t}\) describes the amplitude decay.

Forced harmonic vibration

\[ m\ddot{x}+c\dot{x}+kx=F_0\sin(\omega t) \]
Equation of motion with sinusoidal forcing: Steady-state response has the same frequency as the forcing. The complete response = transient (free vibration) + steady state — for lightly damped systems, the transient decays after a few cycles.
\[ X=\frac{F_0/k}{\sqrt{(1-r^2)^2+(2\zeta r)^2}},\quad r=\frac{\omega}{\omega_n} \]
Steady-state amplitude: \(r\) = frequency ratio. \(F_0/k\) = static deflection under \(F_0\). The denominator = dynamic magnification factor (DMF):
  • \(r \ll 1\): \(X \approx F_0/k\) (quasi-static)
  • \(r = 1\): \(X = F_0/(2\zeta k)\) = resonance — amplitude limited only by damping
  • \(r \gg 1\): \(X \approx F_0/(m\omega^2)\) — mass-dominated, low response
\[ \phi=\arctan\!\left(\frac{2\zeta r}{1-r^2}\right) \]
Phase angle \(\phi\): Response lags the force. \(\phi=0\) at \(r\ll1\) (in phase), \(\phi=90°\) at resonance (\(r=1\)), \(\phi\to180°\) at \(r\gg1\) (out of phase). The 90° phase shift at resonance is what identifies the natural frequency in experimental testing.

Transmissibility and vibration isolation

\[ TR=\frac{F_{transmitted}}{F_{applied}}=\sqrt{\frac{1+(2\zeta r)^2}{(1-r^2)^2+(2\zeta r)^2}} \]
Force transmissibility: At \(r=\sqrt{2}\): \(TR=1\) for all \(\zeta\). For \(r > \sqrt{2}\): \(TR < 1\) — isolation begins. For \(r \gg \sqrt{2}\): \(TR \approx 1/r^2\) — high-frequency isolation. Key insight: Adding damping reduces resonance peak but worsens high-frequency isolation — there is a trade-off.
Design rule: To isolate a machine from a foundation, target \(r = \omega/\omega_n > 2.5\) (i.e., natural frequency of the mount should be < 0.4× the operating frequency). Use low-stiffness mounts (rubber, springs) to lower \(\omega_n\).

Rotating imbalance

\[ X=\frac{me\omega^2/k}{\sqrt{(1-r^2)^2+(2\zeta r)^2}}=\frac{(m_u/m)r^2}{\sqrt{(1-r^2)^2+(2\zeta r)^2}}\cdot e \]
Rotating imbalance response: \(m_u\) = imbalance mass [kg], \(e\) = eccentricity [m] (distance of imbalance mass from rotation axis), \(m\) = total system mass. At high speed (\(r\gg1\)): amplitude \(X \to m_u e/m\) — system moves at the eccentricity of its centre of mass.
