Turbulence Models

Model selection rationale, RANS equations, turbulent kinetic energy, and when LES is necessary.

The closure problem and turbulence modelling

\[ \vec{V}=\bar{V}+\vec{v}',\quad -\rho\overline{v_i'v_j'}=\mu_t\!\left(\frac{\partial\bar{V}_i}{\partial x_j}+\frac{\partial\bar{V}_j}{\partial x_i}\right)-\frac{2}{3}\rho k\delta_{ij} \]
Reynolds decomposition and Boussinesq hypothesis: RANS solves for mean \(\bar{V}\). Fluctuations \(\vec{v}'\) produce Reynolds stresses. The Boussinesq hypothesis models these stresses using eddy viscosity \(\mu_t\) — an additional, flow-dependent viscosity computed by the turbulence model.
\[ \mu_t = \rho C_\mu \frac{k^2}{\varepsilon}\;\text{(k-}\varepsilon\text{)},\quad \mu_t = \rho\frac{k}{\omega}\;\text{(k-}\omega\text{)} \]
Turbulent (eddy) viscosity: \(k\) = turbulent kinetic energy [m²/s²], \(\varepsilon\) = dissipation rate [m²/s³], \(\omega\) = specific dissipation rate [1/s], \(C_\mu=0.09\). \(\mu_t\) can be 10–1000× larger than \(\mu\) in fully turbulent regions.

k-ε model transport equations

\[ \frac{\partial(\rho k)}{\partial t}+\nabla\cdot(\rho\vec{V}k)=\nabla\cdot\!\left[\left(\mu+\frac{\mu_t}{\sigma_k}\right)\nabla k\right]+P_k-\rho\varepsilon \]
k-equation: \(P_k\) = turbulence production by mean shear. Production minus dissipation = net change in turbulent energy. Near walls, production peaks in the buffer layer. \(\sigma_k=1.0\) (standard k-ε).
\[ \frac{\partial(\rho\varepsilon)}{\partial t}+\nabla\cdot(\rho\vec{V}\varepsilon)=\nabla\cdot\!\left[\left(\mu+\frac{\mu_t}{\sigma_\varepsilon}\right)\nabla\varepsilon\right]+C_1\frac{\varepsilon}{k}P_k-C_2\rho\frac{\varepsilon^2}{k} \]
ε-equation: Models the rate at which turbulent kinetic energy is dissipated into heat. Constants: \(C_1=1.44\), \(C_2=1.92\), \(\sigma_\varepsilon=1.3\). The k-ε model is calibrated against simple shear flows — it loses accuracy in flows with strong curvature, separation, or adverse pressure gradients.

ModelStrengthsWeaknessesTypical use
Standard k-εRobust, fast convergence, good for fully turbulent attached flowsPoor in adverse pressure gradient, separation, near-wall regionsInternal duct flows, simple external flows

Default recommendation: Use SST k-ω for most external aerodynamics and separated internal flows. Use Realizable k-ε for fully enclosed turbulent duct flows without separation.

Turbulence inlet boundary conditions

\[ I=\frac{u'}{U}\approx0.16\,Re_{D_h}^{-1/8},\quad k=\frac{3}{2}(UI)^2,\quad \varepsilon=C_\mu^{3/4}\frac{k^{3/2}}{l},\quad l=0.07D_h \]
Turbulence inlet specification: Intensity \(I\) (turbulence intensity): ratio of velocity fluctuation to mean velocity [%]. Typical values: 0.1–1% (clean freestream), 3–5% (pipe flow), 5–10% (after bends/fittings). Hydraulic diameter \(D_h = 4A/P\) for non-circular ducts. Fluent accepts: I + hydraulic diameter, or I + viscosity ratio \(\mu_t/\mu\).
y+ and model compatibility: SST k-ω with automatic wall treatment: target y⁺ ≈ 1. Standard k-ε with standard wall functions: y⁺ = 30–300. Using y⁺ = 1 with standard wall functions wastes resolution without improving accuracy — and vice versa.
