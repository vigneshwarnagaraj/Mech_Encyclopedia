Governing Equations

The mathematical equations behind every solver menu — what each term means and what each equation controls.

Conservation of mass — continuity

\[ \frac{\partial\rho}{\partial t}+\nabla\cdot(\rho\vec{V})=0 \]
Compressible, unsteady continuity: Rate of change of mass density + divergence of mass flux = 0. In incompressible steady flow: \(\nabla\cdot\vec{V}=0\). In Fluent, this appears as the "continuity" residual. It converges last in incompressible solvers because pressure is coupled to it through the pressure-velocity algorithm.

Conservation of momentum — Navier-Stokes

\[ \rho\!\left(\frac{\partial\vec{V}}{\partial t}+\vec{V}\cdot\nabla\vec{V}\right)=-\nabla p+\mu\nabla^2\vec{V}+\rho\vec{g}+\vec{S} \]
Term-by-term:
  • \(\rho\,\partial\vec{V}/\partial t\): unsteady (local) inertia — zero in steady simulations
  • \(\rho(\vec{V}\cdot\nabla\vec{V})\): convective (nonlinear) inertia — always present
  • \(-\nabla p\): pressure gradient — drives the flow
  • \(\mu\nabla^2\vec{V}\): viscous diffusion — damps velocity gradients
  • \(\rho\vec{g}\): body force — enable in Fluent when buoyancy matters
  • \(\vec{S}\): source terms — rotating reference frame, MHD, porous media
Newtonian fluid · incompressible
\[ \frac{\partial(\rho u)}{\partial t}+\frac{\partial(\rho u^2)}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial(\rho uv)}{\partial y}=-\frac{\partial p}{\partial x}+\mu\!\left(\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}+\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2}\right) \]
x-momentum (2D expanded): This is what Fluent actually discretises for each velocity component. The nonlinear term \(\partial(\rho u^2)/\partial x\) is the source of numerical instability at high Re — it requires upwind or higher-order schemes.

Conservation of energy

\[ \rho c_p\!\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial t}+\vec{V}\cdot\nabla T\right)=\nabla\cdot(k\nabla T)+\dot{q}+\Phi \]
Energy equation: Temperature transport. Left side: rate of change + convection. Right side: conduction + heat source + viscous dissipation \(\Phi\) (only significant at very high viscosity or high shear rates). Enable in Fluent when temperature gradients, heat flux BCs, or buoyancy are present.

General scalar transport equation

\[ \frac{\partial(\rho\phi)}{\partial t}+\underbrace{\nabla\cdot(\rho\vec{V}\phi)}_{\text{convection}}=\underbrace{\nabla\cdot(\Gamma\nabla\phi)}_{\text{diffusion}}+\underbrace{S_\phi}_{\text{source}} \]
General scalar transport: \(\phi\) = any transported quantity: temperature \(T\), species mass fraction \(Y\), turbulent kinetic energy \(k\), dissipation \(\varepsilon\), \(\omega\). \(\Gamma\) = diffusivity. Every equation in CFD fits this form — understanding it means understanding the physics of any Fluent model.
Four terms to understand: Transient term (accumulation), convection term (transport by flow), diffusion term (molecular/turbulent spreading), source term (generation/destruction). If a quantity grows in a region, either the source term is positive or net convection/diffusion is bringing it in.

RANS equations

\[ \rho\!\left(\frac{\partial\bar{V}_i}{\partial t}+\bar{V}_j\frac{\partial\bar{V}_i}{\partial x_j}\right)=-\frac{\partial\bar{p}}{\partial x_i}+\frac{\partial}{\partial x_j}\!\left[\mu\frac{\partial\bar{V}_i}{\partial x_j}-\rho\overline{v_i'v_j'}\right]+\rho g_i \]
Reynolds-averaged N-S: Bar = time-averaged quantities. The Reynolds stress tensor \(-\rho\overline{v_i'v_j'}\) appears because time-averaging produces a correlation of fluctuating velocity components. This is the closure problem — turbulence models (k-ε, k-ω, SST) provide this missing information through the Boussinesq hypothesis: \(-\rho\overline{v_i'v_j'} = \mu_t(\partial\bar{V}_i/\partial x_j + \partial\bar{V}_j/\partial x_i) - \tfrac{2}{3}\rho k\delta_{ij}\).
