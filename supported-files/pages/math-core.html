Math Foundations

The mathematical tools underlying all engineering analysis — calculus, differential equations, vector operators, and linear algebra.

Differential calculus

\[ \frac{df}{dx},\quad \frac{\partial f}{\partial x},\quad \frac{d^2f}{dx^2} \]
Ordinary vs partial derivative: Use \(\partial\) when a function depends on multiple variables and you differentiate with respect to one while holding others fixed. Used throughout fluid mechanics, heat transfer, and structural dynamics.
\[ a_n\frac{d^n y}{dt^n}+\cdots+a_1\frac{dy}{dt}+a_0 y = f(t) \]
Linear ODE: Governs vibration (mass-spring-damper), transient heat transfer (lumped capacitance), and control systems. Solution form depends on the roots of the characteristic equation: real roots → exponential; complex roots → oscillatory.

Chain rule and product rule

\[ \frac{d}{dx}[f(g(x))] = f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x) \]
Chain rule: Needed when differentiating composite functions — appears when deriving material derivative in fluid mechanics: \(D\phi/Dt = \partial\phi/\partial t + \vec{V}\cdot\nabla\phi\).

Integration — practical forms

\[ \int e^{ax}\,dx = \frac{e^{ax}}{a}+C,\quad \int \sin(\omega t)\,dt=-\frac{\cos(\omega t)}{\omega}+C \]
Common integrals in dynamics: The exponential form appears in damped vibration and thermal decay. The trigonometric form appears in forced vibration and Fourier analysis.

Vector calculus operators

\[ \nabla\phi = \operatorname{grad}\phi = \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial x}\hat{i}+\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial y}\hat{j}+\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial z}\hat{k} \]
Gradient: Points in the direction of maximum increase of a scalar field \(\phi\). In CFD, \(-\nabla p\) is the pressure gradient driving the flow.
\[ \nabla\cdot\vec{V} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial w}{\partial z} \]
Divergence: Scalar measure of outward flux per unit volume. \(\nabla\cdot\vec{V}=0\) is the incompressible continuity equation — zero divergence means no net mass flux out of any fluid element.
\[ \nabla^2\phi = \frac{\partial^2\phi}{\partial x^2}+\frac{\partial^2\phi}{\partial y^2}+\frac{\partial^2\phi}{\partial z^2} \]
Laplacian: Appears in the diffusion terms of both the heat equation and the Navier-Stokes viscous term \(\mu\nabla^2\vec{V}\). If \(\nabla^2\phi=0\): Laplace equation (steady conduction with no heat generation).
\[ \nabla\times\vec{V} = \vec{\omega} = \operatorname{curl}\vec{V} \]
Curl / vorticity: \(\vec{\omega}\) = vorticity vector [s⁻¹]. Irrotational flow: \(\nabla\times\vec{V}=0\). Used in potential flow theory and vortex dynamics.

Linear algebra — FEA and MDOF vibrations

\[ [K]\{x\}=\{F\} \]
Matrix system: \([K]\) = coefficient / stiffness matrix (\(n\times n\)), \(\{x\}\) = unknowns (\(n\times1\)), \(\{F\}\) = right-hand side. Solved by Gaussian elimination, LU decomposition, or iterative methods. Used in FEA, MDOF vibrations, and discretised CFD systems.

\[ [A]\{x\}=\lambda\{x\} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \det([A]-\lambda[I])=0 \]
Eigenvalue problem: \(\lambda\) = eigenvalue (natural frequency squared in structural problems), \(\{x\}\) = eigenvector (mode shape). Non-trivial solution exists only when the determinant is zero.

\[ \{x\}=[A]^{-1}\{b\} \quad\text{(if } [A] \text{ invertible)} \]
Matrix inversion: In FEA, the global stiffness matrix is large and sparse — never literally inverted. Solvers use factored or iterative methods. In small MDOF problems, direct inversion is fine.

Dot product, cross product

\[ \vec{A}\cdot\vec{B}=|\vec{A}||\vec{B}|\cos\theta,\quad \vec{A}\times\vec{B}=|\vec{A}||\vec{B}|\sin\theta\,\hat{n} \]
Products: Dot product gives work (\(\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{s}\)). Cross product gives moment (\(\vec{r}\times\vec{F}\)) and angular momentum. The direction of \(\vec{A}\times\vec{B}\) follows the right-hand rule.

Fourier series and transform

\[ f(t)=\frac{a_0}{2}+\sum_{n=1}^\infty\left[a_n\cos(n\omega_0 t)+b_n\sin(n\omega_0 t)\right] \]
Fourier series: Any periodic signal can be decomposed into harmonics. Used in vibration analysis to decompose a complex periodic excitation into its frequency components, each of which can be handled as a single-frequency forced vibration.
\[ X(f)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}x(t)\,e^{-j2\pi ft}\,dt \]
Fourier transform: Converts a time-domain signal to frequency domain. In experimental modal analysis and signal processing, the FFT (Fast Fourier Transform) is the discrete implementation. Input is time history; output is frequency spectrum.
