Beams, Shafts & Buckling

Bending stress, shear stress, torsion, deflection formulas, and Euler column buckling.

Beam bending — flexure formula

\[ \sigma_x=\frac{My}{I},\quad \sigma_{max}=\frac{Mc}{I}=\frac{M}{Z} \]
Bending stress: \(M\) = bending moment [N·m], \(y\) = distance from neutral axis [m], \(I\) = second moment of area [m⁴], \(c\) = distance to outermost fibre [m], \(Z=I/c\) = section modulus [m³]. Maximum stress occurs at \(y=c\) (top or bottom fibre).
Euler-Bernoulli beam: plane sections remain plane
\[ \tau_{max}=\frac{VQ}{Ib},\quad Q=\int_{y}^{c}y'\,dA \]
Shear stress in a beam: \(V\) = shear force [N], \(Q\) = first moment of area above the point of interest [m³], \(b\) = width at the point of interest [m]. Maximum shear stress in a rectangular section: \(\tau_{max}=1.5V/A\) (at the neutral axis). In an I-beam: peak shear stress is in the web.
\[ EI\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=M(x),\quad \frac{dV}{dx}=-w(x),\quad \frac{dM}{dx}=V(x) \]
Beam differential equations: \(w\) = distributed load [N/m] (downward positive), \(EI\) = flexural rigidity [N·m²]. Integrating these with boundary conditions gives: shear force \(V\), bending moment \(M\), slope \(dy/dx\), and deflection \(y\).

Torsion

\[ \tau=\frac{Tr}{J},\quad\phi=\frac{TL}{GJ} \]
Torsion formulas (circular section): \(T\) = torque [N·m], \(r\) = radius at point of interest [m], \(J\) = polar second moment of area [m⁴], \(\phi\) = angle of twist [rad], \(G\) = shear modulus [Pa], \(L\) = shaft length [m]. Maximum shear stress at the outer surface (\(r=r_{outer}\)).
\[ J_{solid}=\frac{\pi d^4}{32},\quad J_{hollow}=\frac{\pi(d_o^4-d_i^4)}{32} \]
Polar moment formulas: Hollow shafts are significantly more efficient in torsion — they use less material for nearly the same \(J\). A hollow shaft with \(d_i/d_o=0.8\) has 59% of solid shaft \(J\) but only 36% of its mass.
\[ \tau_{eq}=\frac{1}{Z_p}\sqrt{M^2+T^2},\quad Z_p=\frac{J}{r} \]
Combined bending and torsion: \(\tau_{eq}\) = equivalent shear stress [Pa] used with the maximum shear stress theory. For ductile shafts under combined loading, use the distortion energy (von Mises) check: \(\sigma_v=\sqrt{\sigma_b^2+3\tau^2}\leq\sigma_y\).

Euler column buckling

\[ P_{cr}=\frac{\pi^2EI}{(KL)^2},\quad \lambda=\frac{KL}{r},\quad r=\sqrt{\frac{I}{A}} \]
Critical buckling load: \(K\) = effective length factor, \(\lambda\) = slenderness ratio, \(r\) = radius of gyration [m].

Long slender columns · elastic · no eccentricity · Euler range: λ > 100
\[ \sigma_{cr}=\frac{P_{cr}}{A}=\frac{\pi^2 E}{\lambda^2} \]
Critical buckling stress: Only valid when \(\sigma_{cr} < \sigma_y\) (elastic buckling). If \(\sigma_{cr} \geq \sigma_y\), use inelastic buckling approaches (Johnson formula for intermediate columns: \(\lambda < \pi\sqrt{2E/\sigma_y}\)). In ANSYS: Linear Buckling analysis returns a load multiplier — multiply by applied load to get \(P_{cr}\).
