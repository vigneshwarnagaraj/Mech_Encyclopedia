Boundary Layer & Turbulence

Flat plate boundary layer, transition, wall units, and the fundamentals of turbulent flow structure.

Flat plate boundary layer — laminar (Blasius)

\[ \delta(x)\approx\frac{5x}{\sqrt{Re_x}},\quad Re_x=\frac{Vx}{\nu} \]
Boundary layer thickness: \(\delta\) = thickness [m], \(x\) = distance from leading edge [m]. Grows as \(\sqrt{x}\). At the transition location (\(Re_x \approx 5\times10^5\)): \(\delta \approx 5x/707\).
Laminar · zero pressure gradient · flat plate
\[ \delta^*=\int_0^\infty\left(1-\frac{u}{U}\right)dy,\quad \theta=\int_0^\infty\frac{u}{U}\left(1-\frac{u}{U}\right)dy \]
Displacement and momentum thicknesses: \(\delta^*\) [m] = effective reduction in flow area due to boundary layer. \(\theta\) [m] = measure of momentum deficit. Shape factor \(H=\delta^*/\theta\): laminar \(H\approx2.59\), turbulent \(H\approx1.3\)–1.5.
\[ \tau_w=\mu\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\bigg|_{y=0},\quad C_f=\frac{\tau_w}{\tfrac{1}{2}\rho V^2} \]
Wall shear stress and skin friction coefficient: \(\tau_w\) [Pa] = shear force per unit area on the wall. Laminar flat plate: \(C_f = 0.664/\sqrt{Re_x}\). Critical for drag estimation and near-wall mesh requirements in CFD.

Transition and turbulence

\[ Re_{x,crit}\approx 5\times10^5\;\text{(flat plate)},\quad Re_{D,crit}\approx 2300\;\text{(pipe)} \]
Critical Reynolds numbers: These are engineering rules of thumb. Actual transition depends on surface roughness, freestream turbulence intensity, and geometry. In CFD, the Gamma-ReTheta (γ-Reθ) transition model captures the transition region explicitly.
\[ u_\tau=\sqrt{\frac{\tau_w}{\rho}},\quad y^+=\frac{u_\tau y}{\nu} \]
Wall units (friction velocity and y+): \(u_\tau\) = friction velocity [m/s]. \(y^+\) = dimensionless distance from wall. Controls which near-wall treatment is applied in CFD:
  • Wall-resolved (k-ω, LES): target \(y^+ \approx 1\)
  • Standard wall functions (k-ε): \(y^+ = 30\)–300
  • Enhanced wall treatment: \(y^+ < 5\)

Turbulent boundary layer (turbulent flat plate)

\[ \delta_{turb}\approx\frac{0.37x}{Re_x^{1/5}},\quad C_{f,turb}\approx\frac{0.074}{Re_x^{1/5}} \]
Turbulent boundary layer growth: Grows faster than laminar (\(x^{0.8}\) vs \(x^{0.5}\)). Turbulent skin friction is also higher but the profile is fuller — less likely to separate under adverse pressure gradient.

Turbulence kinetic energy and energy cascade

\[ k=\tfrac{1}{2}\overline{u_i'u_i'},\quad \varepsilon=\nu\overline{\frac{\partial u_i'}{\partial x_j}\frac{\partial u_i'}{\partial x_j}} \]
Turbulent kinetic energy \(k\) [m²/s²]: Kinetic energy of velocity fluctuations per unit mass. Dissipation rate \(\varepsilon\) [m²/s³] = rate at which \(k\) is converted to thermal energy at the smallest scales (Kolmogorov scales). These are the two quantities transported in the k-ε model.
\[ l_K=\left(\frac{\nu^3}{\varepsilon}\right)^{1/4},\quad \tau_K=\left(\frac{\nu}{\varepsilon}\right)^{1/2} \]
Kolmogorov microscales: \(l_K\) = smallest eddy length scale [m], \(\tau_K\) = smallest eddy time scale [s]. In LES, the mesh must resolve scales close to \(l_K\) to capture all turbulence. In RANS (k-ε, k-ω), all scales are modelled.
