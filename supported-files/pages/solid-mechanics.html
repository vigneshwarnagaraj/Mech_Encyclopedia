Stress & Strain Basics

Definitions of stress and strain, Hooke's law, elastic constants, generalized 3D relations, and thermal strain.

Stress definitions

\[ \sigma=\frac{F}{A},\quad\tau=\frac{V}{A} \]
Normal stress \(\sigma\) and shear stress \(\tau\): [Pa = N/m²]. \(F\) = axial force [N], \(V\) = shear force [N], \(A\) = cross-sectional area [m²]. Tension positive, compression negative (common convention). In FEA: ANSYS reports stresses in MPa; check unit system is consistent.
Pa or MPa
\[ \sigma_{ij}=\begin{bmatrix}\sigma_x&\tau_{xy}&\tau_{xz}\\\tau_{yx}&\sigma_y&\tau_{yz}\\\tau_{zx}&\tau_{zy}&\sigma_z\end{bmatrix} \]
Stress tensor (3D): 9 components, symmetric (\(\tau_{ij}=\tau_{ji}\)) → 6 independent. Each component \(\sigma_{ij}\) = stress on face with normal in direction \(i\), acting in direction \(j\). FEA outputs all 6 components plus derived quantities (von Mises, principal stresses).

Strain definitions

\[ \varepsilon=\frac{\Delta L}{L_0},\quad\gamma=\frac{\delta}{h}\approx\tan\phi \]
Normal strain \(\varepsilon\) and shear strain \(\gamma\): Dimensionless [m/m]. \(\varepsilon\) = change in length / original length. \(\gamma\) = angular distortion [rad]. Tensile strain positive. Engineering shear strain \(\gamma_{xy} = 2\varepsilon_{xy}\) (factor of 2 vs. tensor shear strain — be aware of this in FEA output).
\[ \varepsilon_x=\frac{\partial u}{\partial x},\quad \varepsilon_y=\frac{\partial v}{\partial y},\quad \gamma_{xy}=\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial v}{\partial x} \]
Strain-displacement relations (2D): \(u, v\) = displacement components [m]. These are the kinematic equations that relate deformation to strain — the foundation of all FEA element formulations.

Hooke's law — linear elasticity

\[ \sigma=E\varepsilon,\quad\tau=G\gamma \]
Hooke's law (1D): \(E\) = Young's modulus [Pa], \(G\) = shear modulus [Pa]. Steel: \(E\approx200\) GPa, \(G\approx79\) GPa. Aluminum: \(E\approx69\) GPa. Valid only in the elastic (linear) region — below yield stress.
Linear elastic · isotropic · below yield stress
\[ E=2G(1+\nu),\quad K=\frac{E}{3(1-2\nu)},\quad G=\frac{E}{2(1+\nu)} \]
Relations between elastic constants: \(\nu\) = Poisson's ratio [-], \(K\) = bulk modulus [Pa]. For steel: \(\nu\approx0.30\). For rubber: \(\nu\approx0.5\) (incompressible). \(\nu=0.5\) means \(K\to\infty\) — volume is preserved under hydrostatic stress.
\[ \varepsilon_{lateral}=-\nu\varepsilon_{axial} \]
Poisson effect: A tensile strain in the axial direction produces compressive strains in the lateral directions, scaled by \(\nu\). Important for constrained expansion (e.g., a layer bonded between rigid plates) — the lateral constraint creates biaxial stress.

Generalised Hooke's law (3D)

\[ \varepsilon_x=\frac{1}{E}\left[\sigma_x-\nu(\sigma_y+\sigma_z)\right],\quad \gamma_{xy}=\frac{\tau_{xy}}{G} \]
3D stress-strain relations: Each normal strain depends on all three normal stresses via Poisson coupling. Shear strains depend only on the corresponding shear stress. In matrix form: \(\{\varepsilon\}=[D]^{-1}\{\sigma\}\), where \([D]\) is the constitutive (stiffness) matrix used in FEA element formulation.
\[ \{\sigma\}=[D]\{\varepsilon\},\quad [D]=\frac{E}{(1+\nu)(1-2\nu)}\begin{bmatrix}1-\nu&\nu&\nu&\cdots\\\nu&1-\nu&\nu&\cdots\\\vdots&&&\ddots\end{bmatrix} \]
Constitutive matrix \([D]\): Maps strain to stress. Full \(6\times6\) symmetric matrix for isotropic linear elastic material. In FEA: \([k]^e=\int[B]^T[D][B]\,dV\) — the element stiffness is the integral of \([D]\) weighted by the strain-displacement matrix \([B]\).

Thermal strain

\[ \varepsilon_{th}=\alpha_{th}\Delta T,\quad\sigma_{th}=-E\alpha_{th}\Delta T\;\text{(fully constrained)} \]
Thermal strain: \(\alpha_{th}\) = coefficient of thermal expansion [K⁻¹]. Steel: \(\alpha_{th}\approx12\times10^{-6}\) K⁻¹. Aluminum: \(\alpha_{th}\approx23\times10^{-6}\) K⁻¹. If free to expand: strain only, no stress. If fully constrained: stress proportional to \(E\alpha_{th}\Delta T\) (compressive for heating).
ANSYS thermal-structural coupling: Run a Steady-State Thermal analysis first, then link the temperature result as an input to Static Structural. The thermal expansion will be applied automatically using the material's \(\alpha_{th}\) values.
