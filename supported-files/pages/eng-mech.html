Engineering Mechanics

Static and dynamic equilibrium, rotation, inertia, and friction — the direct prerequisites for structural FEA and vibration analysis.

Static equilibrium

\[ \sum F_x=0,\quad\sum F_y=0,\quad\sum M_O=0 \]
2D rigid body equilibrium: Three independent equations. Sum of forces in each direction and sum of moments about any point must all equal zero. In 3D: 6 equations (\(\sum F_x, \sum F_y, \sum F_z, \sum M_x, \sum M_y, \sum M_z\)).
\[ M=F\,d_\perp = F r\sin\theta \]
Moment of a force: \(M\) [N·m], \(d_\perp\) = perpendicular distance from line of action to pivot [m], \(\theta\) = angle between force and moment arm. Sign convention: counterclockwise positive (unless stated otherwise).
\[ \sum M_A = 0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad R_B = \frac{\sum F_i \cdot x_i}{L} \]
Simply supported beam reactions: Take moments about one support to find the other. A classic first step before computing shear force and bending moment diagrams.

Friction

\[ F_f \leq \mu_s N \quad\text{(static)},\quad F_f = \mu_k N \quad\text{(kinetic)} \]
Coulomb friction: \(\mu_s\) = static friction coefficient, \(\mu_k\) = kinetic friction coefficient, \(N\) = normal force [N]. Typical values: steel on steel dry: \(\mu_s \approx 0.74\). In FEA contact: friction coefficient is a required input for frictional contact pairs.

Rotation and inertia

\[ \sum M = I\alpha,\quad KE_{rot}=\tfrac{1}{2}I\omega^2,\quad P_{rot}=T\omega \]
Rotational dynamics: \(I\) = mass moment of inertia [kg·m²], \(\alpha\) = angular acceleration [rad/s²], \(\omega\) = angular velocity [rad/s], \(T\) = torque [N·m]. Analogy with translational: \(I\leftrightarrow m\), \(\omega\leftrightarrow v\), \(T\leftrightarrow F\).
\[ I=\int r^2\,dm,\quad I_{parallel} = I_{cm}+md^2 \]
Moment of inertia and parallel axis theorem: \(I_{cm}\) = inertia about centroidal axis, \(d\) = distance to parallel axis, \(m\) = mass. Used to shift the reference axis for compound bodies.

Virtual work principle

\[ \delta W = \sum \vec{F}_i \cdot \delta\vec{r}_i = 0 \quad\text{(equilibrium)} \]
Principle of virtual work: A system is in equilibrium if the total virtual work done by all applied forces through any virtual displacement is zero. This is the theoretical foundation of the finite element method — the weak form of the governing equations is derived from this principle.

Second moments of area (for beams)

\[ I_{xx}=\int y^2\,dA,\quad I_{yy}=\int x^2\,dA,\quad J=I_{xx}+I_{yy} \]
Second moment of area: \(I\) [m⁴]. Used in bending stress \(\sigma=My/I\) and deflection formulas. \(J\) = polar second moment of area [m⁴] — used in torsion \(\tau=Tr/J\).
Rectangle (b×h): \(I_{xx}=bh^3/12\). Circle (diameter d): \(I=\pi d^4/64\). Hollow circle: \(I=\pi(d_o^4-d_i^4)/64\).
