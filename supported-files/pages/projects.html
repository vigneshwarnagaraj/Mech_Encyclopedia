Projects & Professional Use

Using this manual in real engineering work — simulation validation, project workflow, and professional reporting standards.

Simulation project workflow

  1. Define the objective clearly: What quantity do you need? (pressure drop, max stress, natural frequency, heat transfer rate). This determines your mesh refinement focus, convergence monitor, and validation approach.
  2. Hand-calculate a sanity check: Before running any simulation, estimate the answer analytically or from a correlation. If your CFD result is 10× the hand calculation — investigate before spending compute time.
  3. Start simple: 2D before 3D. Coarse mesh before fine. Steady before transient. Laminar before turbulent. Add complexity only when the simpler model fails to capture the physics.
  4. Document your setup: Record all BCs, material properties, solver settings, mesh statistics, and turbulence model choice. Future you will thank present you.
  5. Run mesh independence: Three levels minimum. Report the GCI or percentage change. Never report a single-mesh result as validated.
  6. Validate against something: Analytical solution, published correlation, published experimental data, or a known benchmark case. At minimum, check mass and energy conservation.
  7. Report integrated quantities: Drag, pressure drop, peak stress, heat transfer rate — not just contour images. Tables of numbers allow comparison across cases.

Interview-ready formula list

These are the formulas to write from memory in a technical interview — without looking them up:

  • Newton's 2nd law · Bernoulli equation · continuity equation
  • Navier-Stokes (concept — not full tensor index form)
  • Fourier conduction law · Newton's law of cooling
  • Hooke's law · von Mises stress definition and physical meaning
  • Natural frequency SDOF · damping ratio definition
  • Reynolds number · Prandtl number · Nusselt number relation
  • Drag / lift force equation · FEA global equation [K]{u} = {F}
Interview tip: Know what each formula tells you physically. "Reynolds number is the ratio of inertial to viscous forces" is worth more than reciting the formula alone.

Simulation validation checklist

  • Compare to an analytical solution, published experiment, or textbook benchmark problem.
  • Check mass balance (inlet = outlet ± 0.1%).
  • Check energy balance if thermal analysis is involved.
  • Run at least 3 mesh densities and confirm mesh independence.
  • Verify boundary conditions are physically correct — not just numerically stable.
  • Plot velocity or temperature profile through cross-sections — does the shape make physical sense?
  • Check reaction forces equal applied loads (FEA).
  • Report factor of safety with location and loading case (structural).
  • If something looks too smooth or too sharp — investigate before reporting it.
