Dimensionless Numbers

These numbers tell you which physical effects dominate and which can be neglected. Knowing them physically — not just as formulas — is what separates engineering understanding from formula lookup.

Core dimensionless numbers

NumberFormulaPhysical meaningKey ranges
Reynolds (Re)\(\dfrac{\rho VL}{\mu}=\dfrac{VL}{\nu}\)Inertia / viscous forces<2300 laminar pipe; >5×10⁵ turbulent flat plate
Mach (Ma)\(\dfrac{V}{a}\)Flow speed / speed of sound<0.3 incompressible; >1 supersonic
Froude (Fr)\(\dfrac{V}{\sqrt{gL}}\)Inertia / gravity (surface waves)Ship design, open channel flow
Weber (We)\(\dfrac{\rho V^2 L}{\sigma}\)Inertia / surface tensionDroplets, sprays, thin films
Euler (Eu)\(\dfrac{\Delta p}{\rho V^2}\)Pressure / inertia forcesCavitation index: \(\sigma_c=(p-p_v)/q\)

Physical interpretation — interview standard

Interview tip: Know what each number means as a ratio of forces, timescales, or diffusivities. "Reynolds number is the ratio of inertial to viscous forces" is worth more than reciting the formula.
NumberPlain English interpretation
ReHow hard the flow pushes (inertia) versus how hard the fluid resists shearing (viscosity). High Re → turbulent.
MaHow fast you move relative to sound. Ma > 0.3 → density changes matter → incompressible assumption breaks down.
PrWhich diffuses faster — momentum or heat? Pr ≪ 1 (liquid metals): heat diffuses quickly. Pr ≫ 1 (oils): momentum diffuses quickly, thermal BL is thin inside velocity BL.
NuHow many times more heat is transferred by convection than would be by conduction alone in the same fluid layer. Nu = 1 → pure conduction.
StRatio of oscillation period to flow-through time. St ≈ 0.2 for vortex shedding from a cylinder — used to predict shedding frequency \(f = StV/D\).
BiExternal resistance to heat removal versus internal resistance to heat conduction. Small Bi → body temperature nearly uniform → lumped model valid.

Buckingham π theorem

\[ \text{Number of } \pi\text{ groups} = n - m \]
Buckingham π theorem: \(n\) = number of variables in the problem, \(m\) = number of independent fundamental dimensions (usually 3: M, L, T; or 4 if temperature included). Each \(\pi\) group is a dimensionless combination of the original variables. Used to design scale model experiments and correlate empirical data.
Practical use: If your CFD or experiment has 5 variables and 3 dimensions → 2 dimensionless groups → plot one vs. the other to get a universal correlation.
