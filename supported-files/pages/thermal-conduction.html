Conduction

Fourier's law, 1D steady conduction, thermal resistance networks, and transient conduction including lumped capacitance.

Fourier's law of heat conduction

\[ \dot{Q}=-kA\frac{dT}{dx} \]
1D Fourier's law: \(k\) = thermal conductivity [W/m·K], \(A\) = cross-sectional area [m²], \(dT/dx\) = temperature gradient [K/m]. Negative sign: heat flows from hot to cold (positive \(x\) direction, decreasing \(T\)).
W (watts)Steady · 1D · isotropic material
\[ \vec{q}''=-k\nabla T\quad[\text{W/m}^2] \]
Heat flux vector (3D): \(\vec{q}''\) = heat flux [W/m²]. In CFD, the energy equation uses this as the conduction term. In FEA (ANSYS Steady-State Thermal), nodal temperatures are solved and heat flux is derived.

Heat diffusion equation

\[ \nabla^2T+\frac{\dot{q}}{k}=\frac{1}{\alpha}\frac{\partial T}{\partial t} \]
General heat diffusion equation: \(\dot{q}\) = volumetric heat generation [W/m³], \(\alpha = k/(\rho c_p)\) = thermal diffusivity [m²/s].
Special cases:
  • Steady, no generation: \(\nabla^2T=0\) (Laplace equation)
  • Steady with generation: \(\nabla^2T+\dot{q}/k=0\) (Poisson equation)
  • Transient, no generation: \(\partial T/\partial t = \alpha\nabla^2T\)

Thermal resistance networks

\[ R_{cond}=\frac{L}{kA},\quad R_{conv}=\frac{1}{hA},\quad R_{rad}\approx\frac{1}{4\varepsilon\sigma T^3 A} \]
Thermal resistances: Analogous to electrical resistors. Resistances in series add directly; in parallel they add as reciprocals. \(L\) = wall thickness [m], \(h\) = convection coefficient [W/m²·K].
\[ R_{cyl}=\frac{\ln(r_2/r_1)}{2\pi k L},\quad R_{sphere}=\frac{1}{4\pi k}\left(\frac{1}{r_1}-\frac{1}{r_2}\right) \]
Cylindrical and spherical resistances: Used for insulated pipes, reactor walls, nuclear fuel elements. \(r_1\) = inner radius, \(r_2\) = outer radius, \(L\) = cylinder length.
\[ \dot{Q}=\frac{T_{hot}-T_{cold}}{R_{total}},\quad R_{total}=\sum_{\text{series}} R_i \]
Heat flow through resistance network: Analogous to Ohm's law: \(\dot{Q}\) ↔ current, \(\Delta T\) ↔ voltage, \(R\) ↔ resistance. A multi-layer wall has resistances in series; parallel heat paths add in parallel.

Transient conduction

Lumped capacitance (Biot number check first)

\[ Bi=\frac{hL_c}{k},\quad L_c=\frac{V}{A_s} \]
Biot number: Ratio of external convection resistance to internal conduction resistance. If \(Bi < 0.1\), internal temperature gradients are small → body is nearly isothermal → lumped capacitance is valid.
Lumped valid only if Bi < 0.1
\[ \frac{T(t)-T_\infty}{T_i-T_\infty}=e^{-t/\tau_t},\quad \tau_t=\frac{\rho V c_p}{hA_s}=\frac{\rho L_c c_p}{h} \]
Lumped capacitance response: Exponential decay toward ambient temperature. \(\tau_t\) = thermal time constant [s]. At \(t=\tau_t\), the temperature difference has reduced to \(1/e \approx 37\%\) of the initial difference. At \(t=5\tau_t\), essentially at steady state.
\[ Fo=\frac{\alpha t}{L_c^2} \]
Fourier number: Dimensionless time for transient conduction. Represents heat conducted out relative to heat stored. Used with Heisler charts for cases where \(Bi \geq 0.1\). Fo > 0.2: single-term series solution is accurate.
Practical note: In ANSYS Steady-State Thermal, time is not solved — you get the equilibrium temperature field. For temperature evolution over time, switch to Transient Thermal analysis and set the time step.
