Fluid Mechanics · 05
Flow Equations

Continuity, Bernoulli, Euler, Navier-Stokes, and the integral momentum equation.

Continuity equation — mass conservation

\[ \frac{\partial\rho}{\partial t}+\nabla\cdot(\rho\vec{V})=0 \]
General continuity (compressible, unsteady): Conservation of mass in differential form. Rate of change of density + net mass flux out = 0. Every CFD solver solves this at every cell every iteration.
\[ \nabla\cdot\vec{V}=0 \quad\text{(incompressible steady)} \]
Incompressible continuity: Density is constant, so the divergence of velocity is zero. Valid for Ma < 0.3 in gases, and almost always for liquids.
ρ = constantSteady or unsteady
\[ A_1V_1=A_2V_2,\quad Q=AV,\quad\dot{m}=\rho AV \]
1D steady incompressible continuity: \(Q\) = volumetric flow rate [m³/s], \(\dot{m}\) = mass flow rate [kg/s]. If cross-section halves, velocity doubles. Used for pipe networks, nozzles, and duct sizing.

Bernoulli equation

\[ \frac{p}{\rho g}+\frac{V^2}{2g}+z=H=\text{const along a streamline} \]
Bernoulli (head form): \(H\) = total head [m]. Three contributions: pressure head + velocity head + elevation head. Used for pipe flow, nozzles, orifices, Pitot tubes, and venturi meters.
Steady · incompressible · inviscid · along one streamline
\[ p_1+\tfrac{1}{2}\rho V_1^2+\rho g z_1 = p_2+\tfrac{1}{2}\rho V_2^2+\rho g z_2 \]
Pressure form: Dynamic pressure \(q=\tfrac{1}{2}\rho V^2\) [Pa]. Total pressure \(p_0 = p + q\) = stagnation pressure. Used in Pitot-static probes: \(V=\sqrt{2(p_0-p)/\rho}\).
\[ p_1+\tfrac{1}{2}\rho V_1^2+\rho g z_1 = p_2+\tfrac{1}{2}\rho V_2^2+\rho g z_2 + \rho g h_L \]
Bernoulli with head loss: \(h_L\) = head loss due to friction and minor losses [m]. This is the practical form for real pipe systems. The term \(\rho g h_L\) = pressure drop due to losses.

Euler equation (inviscid momentum)

\[ \rho\left(\frac{\partial\vec{V}}{\partial t}+\vec{V}\cdot\nabla\vec{V}\right)=-\nabla p+\rho\vec{g} \]
Euler equation: Navier-Stokes without the viscous term. Suitable for ideal (inviscid) flow. The nonlinear convective term \(\vec{V}\cdot\nabla\vec{V}\) is the source of all the difficulty in CFD — it is responsible for turbulence, shock formation, and flow instability.
Inviscid flow (no viscosity)

Navier-Stokes equations

\[ \rho\!\left(\frac{\partial\vec{V}}{\partial t}+\vec{V}\cdot\nabla\vec{V}\right)=-\nabla p+\mu\nabla^2\vec{V}+\rho\vec{g}+\vec{S} \]
Navier-Stokes (incompressible):
  • \(\rho\,\partial\vec{V}/\partial t\) = unsteady inertia
  • \(\rho(\vec{V}\cdot\nabla\vec{V})\) = convective inertia (nonlinear)
  • \(-\nabla p\) = pressure gradient driving the flow
  • \(\mu\nabla^2\vec{V}\) = viscous diffusion
  • \(\rho\vec{g}\) = gravitational body force
  • \(\vec{S}\) = other source terms (rotation, Coriolis, etc.)
Newtonian · incompressible
What CFD solves: Fluent solves the time-averaged (RANS) or spatially-filtered (LES) form of N-S, where turbulent fluctuations are modelled rather than resolved directly. The menu item "Viscous Model" selects how those fluctuations are handled.

Material derivative

\[ \frac{D\phi}{Dt}=\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial t}+\vec{V}\cdot\nabla\phi \]
Material (substantial) derivative: Rate of change of property \(\phi\) following a fluid particle. First term = local change at a fixed point; second term = change due to the particle moving through a gradient. All governing equations are written in this form.

Integral momentum equation (control volume)

\[ \sum\vec{F}=\frac{d}{dt}\int_{CV}\rho\vec{V}\,dV+\oint_{CS}\rho\vec{V}(\vec{V}\cdot\hat{n})\,dA \]
Reynolds Transport Theorem for momentum: Used for control volume analysis — computing forces on nozzles, pipe bends, turbine blades, and vehicles without solving the full differential equations. In steady flow, the first term vanishes.
