Basic Physics Formulas

Core physical relationships behind all engineering calculations — from Newton's laws to energy conservation.

Kinematics

\[ v=\frac{dx}{dt},\quad a=\frac{dv}{dt}=\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} \]
Symbols: \(x\) = displacement [m], \(v\) = velocity [m/s], \(a\) = acceleration [m/s²], \(t\) = time [s]. These are definitions, not assumptions.
\[ v=u+at,\quad s=ut+\tfrac{1}{2}at^2,\quad v^2=u^2+2as \]
SUVAT equations: \(u\) = initial velocity [m/s], \(s\) = displacement [m], \(a\) = acceleration [m/s²]. Valid only for constant acceleration. Do not apply to variable-acceleration problems — use integration instead.
Constant acceleration only

Circular / rotational kinematics

\[ v=r\omega,\quad a_t=r\alpha,\quad a_n=\frac{v^2}{r}=r\omega^2 \]
Symbols: \(r\) = radius [m], \(\omega\) = angular velocity [rad/s], \(\alpha\) = angular acceleration [rad/s²]. \(a_t\) = tangential acceleration, \(a_n\) = centripetal acceleration (directed inward).

Forces, Newton's Laws

\[ \vec{F}=m\vec{a} \]
Newton's second law: \(F\) [N], \(m\) [kg], \(a\) [m/s²]. This is the governing idea behind all dynamic structural and flow analysis — the Navier-Stokes equation is simply this law applied to a fluid element.
\[ \text{Every action has an equal and opposite reaction: }\vec{F}_{12}=-\vec{F}_{21} \]
Newton's third law: In FEA, this is why reaction forces must equal applied loads in a static analysis — the structure exerts equal and opposite forces on the supports.

Work, Energy, Power

\[ W=\int\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{s},\quad KE=\tfrac{1}{2}mv^2,\quad PE_{grav}=mgh \]
Energy: \(W\) = work [J], \(KE\) = kinetic energy [J], \(PE\) = gravitational potential energy [J], \(g=9.81\) m/s², \(h\) = height above datum [m].
\[ PE_{spring}=\tfrac{1}{2}kx^2,\quad P=\frac{dW}{dt}=\vec{F}\cdot\vec{v} \]
Spring potential energy and power: \(k\) = spring stiffness [N/m], \(x\) = deformation [m], \(P\) = power [W]. Spring PE is the basis of all FEA strain energy calculations.
\[ E_1+W_{12}=E_2 \quad\text{(energy conservation)} \]
Conservation of energy: Total mechanical energy is conserved in the absence of friction and damping. In thermal systems, add heat transfer terms. In fluid systems, this becomes Bernoulli's equation.

Momentum and Impulse

\[ \vec{p}=m\vec{v},\quad \vec{J}=\int\vec{F}\,dt=\Delta\vec{p} \]
Linear momentum: \(\vec{p}\) [kg·m/s], \(\vec{J}\) = impulse [N·s]. In CFD, the integral momentum equation on a control volume is the basis for computing forces on bodies from the surrounding flow field.
\[ \vec{L}=I\vec{\omega},\quad \vec{M}=\frac{d\vec{L}}{dt}=I\vec{\alpha} \]
Angular momentum: \(\vec{L}\) [kg·m²/s], \(\vec{M}\) = moment / torque [N·m], \(I\) = mass moment of inertia [kg·m²]. Used in rotational dynamics and gyroscope analysis.

Waves and Oscillations

\[ \lambda f = c,\quad T=\frac{1}{f},\quad \omega=2\pi f \]
Wave relations: \(\lambda\) = wavelength [m], \(f\) = frequency [Hz], \(c\) = wave speed [m/s], \(T\) = period [s], \(\omega\) = angular frequency [rad/s]. Same relations used for mechanical vibrations, acoustic waves, and electromagnetic waves.
\[ \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} = c^2\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} \]
1D wave equation: \(u\) = displacement, \(c\) = wave speed. Governs longitudinal waves in rods (\(c=\sqrt{E/\rho}\)), transverse waves in strings, and acoustic pressure waves.
